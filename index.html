Australia’s Strength Boom

The rise of fitness, gym culture, and organised strength training in Australia.

From casual workouts to organised lifting

Strength training has become a visible part of Australian fitness culture. This visualisation explores who participates in Fitness/Gym activities, why people train, where organised participation is strongest, and how recreational strength activity connects to organised powerlifting.

Who is driving the boom?

Younger adults and strong female participation shape Australia’s current gym culture. The first section shows who participates and which strength-related activities dominate.

Young adults are driving Australia’s gym culture

Fitness and gym participation is strongest among younger Australian adults, especially those aged 18–24 and 25–34.

Figure 1. Fitness/Gym participation is strongest among younger adults, with the highest rate recorded for those aged 18–24.

Gym workouts dominate muscle-strengthening activity

Among common muscle-strengthening activities, gym workouts stand out clearly. This supports the idea that strength training has become part of everyday fitness culture.

Figure 2. Gym workouts are the most common muscle-strengthening activity, well ahead of other activities such as running and swimming.

Why do Australians train?

The data shows that fitness is connected to health, enjoyment, achievement, and personal goals, while barriers such as injury, age, and time still limit participation.

Australians train for health, enjoyment, and achievement

The leading motivations show that gym and strength training are not only about sport. They are connected to physical health, enjoyment, personal achievement, and training goals.

Figure 3. Australians mainly participate for health, enjoyment, and personal achievement rather than purely competitive reasons.

Injury, age, and time still limit participation

Even though fitness culture is growing, some Australians still face barriers. Poor health, injury, age, disability, and lack of time are among the most common reasons for not participating.

Figure 4. Poor health or injury is the most common barrier to participation, followed by age-related limitations and low motivation.

Where is participation strongest?

State and territory differences show that organised Fitness/Gym participation is not evenly distributed. ACT and the Northern Territory stand out in the data.

Organised Fitness/Gym participation varies across Australia

This map shows organised Fitness/Gym participation by state and territory. Darker regions represent higher participation rates.

Figure 5. Organised Fitness/Gym participation varies across Australia, with stronger participation in ACT and the Northern Territory.

Organised Fitness/Gym participation is highest in ACT and NT

Participation differs across Australia. ACT records the highest organised Fitness/Gym participation, followed by the Northern Territory, while Tasmania records the lowest rate.

How does gym culture connect to powerlifting?

Recreational strength training connects to organised lifting communities and competition records. Powerlifting data shows how strength culture has grown beyond casual gym participation.

Muscle-strengthening participation increased before 2018

AIHW data shows that the proportion of adults meeting the muscle-strengthening guideline rose between 2016 and 2018 for both males and females.

Figure 8. The proportion of adults meeting muscle-strengthening guidelines increased between 2016 and 2018 for both males and females.

Powerlifting records show a strong rise after 2010

OpenPowerlifting records show strong growth in Australian powerlifting entries from 2010 to 2025. The shaded area highlights the COVID-era disruption, followed by a strong rebound and a peak in 2024.

Figure 9. Australian powerlifting records grew strongly after 2010, dipped during the COVID period, and then rebounded sharply to a peak in 2024.

Powerlifting records remain male-heavy, but female participation is substantial

OpenPowerlifting records from 2010 to 2025 show that male lifters make up the largest share of Australian records. However, female lifters still account for a substantial portion of entries, showing that organised strength sport is not only a male space.

Figure 10. Powerlifting remains male-dominated, but female lifters still represent a substantial share of recorded participation.

From gym participation to organised strength sport

The overall story connects three layers of strength culture: everyday Fitness/Gym participation, muscle-strengthening habits, and organised powerlifting records. This flow diagram summarises how recreational training can feed into structured lifting communities.

Figure 11. This summary diagram links mainstream Fitness/Gym participation, muscle-strengthening habits, and organised powerlifting culture.

Final takeaway

Australia’s strength boom is not only about competitive lifting. The data shows that gym workouts are a leading muscle-strengthening activity, younger adults are especially active, women play a major role in organised Fitness/Gym participation, and powerlifting records have grown sharply since 2010. Together, these patterns show how strength training has moved from a niche activity into mainstream Australian fitness culture.
